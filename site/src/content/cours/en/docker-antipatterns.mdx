## Security: don't disarm isolation

**Mounting the Docker socket inside a container.** `-v /var/run/docker.sock:/var/run/docker.sock` gives the container **full control of the daemon** тАФ therefore, in effect, **root on the host**: all it takes is launching a privileged container that mounts `/`. It is one of the most common escapes (and it is why sandboxes such as play-with-docker run as *Docker-in-Docker*, not with the host's socket).

**`--privileged` and `--cap-add ALL`.** `--privileged` strips away most of the isolation (access to *devices*, to almost every *capability*). You almost never need it; if a container truly needs **one** specific capability, add it **surgically** (`--cap-add NET_ADMIN`) and drop the rest (`--cap-drop ALL`).

**Running as root inside the container** (seen in the [basics course](../docker-bases/)): a non-privileged `USER` should be the norm, and the daemon's *rootless* mode goes further still.

<Note type="danger" title="Secrets baked into the image">
  A secret passed via `ENV` or `ARG` **stays written in the layers**: `docker history --no-trunc` reveals it, and it travels with the image on the registry. **Our own lab is guilty of this**: `ENV MYSQL_PASSWORD=tempo` ends up in the image. In production: secrets **at runtime** (variables injected at launch, *Docker/Swarm secrets*, mounted files) or, at build time, `RUN --mount=type=secret` (BuildKit) тАФ never hard-coded in the Dockerfile.
</Note>

**Trusting just any image.** An image pulled from an unknown registry can contain anything. Prefer **official** or signed images, **pin** by digest, and **scan** your images (`docker scout`, Trivy, Grype) before deploying.

## Building cleanly

**Breaking the `apt` cache.** `RUN apt-get update` then `RUN apt-get install тАж` as **two** separate instructions: the day you add a package, Docker reuses a **cached, stale** `update` and installs outdated versions. **Always** put `update && install` in the **same** `RUN`.

**Cleaning up in a different layer from the one that made the mess.** Running `rm -rf /var/lib/apt/lists/*` in a **later** `RUN` does nothing for size: the previous layer already holds those files, and a layer never erases the contents of the one below. Cleanup must live in the **same** `RUN` as the install.

**`latest` in production.** `latest` is a moving tag: two `pull`s can deliver two different images. For production, **pin** a version (`postgres:16.3`) or, better, a **digest** (`postgres@sha256:тАж`) тАФ the only truly immutable reference.

**`COPY . .` without a `.dockerignore`.** You then send the whole context to the daemon: `.git`, `node_modules`, secret files, build artifactsтАж A `.dockerignore` (like `.gitignore`) shrinks the context, speeds up the build and **avoids baking in secrets** by accident.

**`ADD` where `COPY` is enough.** `ADD` has surprising behaviours: it automatically **unpacks** local tar archives and can **download** URLs. Use `COPY` by default; reserve `ADD` for explicit, documented cases.

<Note type="warning" title="Nothing superfluous in the final image">
  `vim`, `curl`, compilersтАж bloat the image and **widen the attack surface**. Our lab installs `vim` for teaching purposes; in production you do without тАФ and the [multi-stage build](../docker-images/) exists precisely to ship only what is needed.
</Note>

## Actually running it

<Note type="danger" title="The PID 1 problem">
  A container's first process (PID 1) has a special role: it must **forward signals** and **reap zombie processes**. A `bash -c "тАж"`, a `sleep infinity` or a carelessly launched server **do none of this**: `docker stop` then waits 10 s and **kills brutally** (SIGKILL), while zombies pile up. **Our lab illustrates this** (`sleep infinity`, `mysqld_safe`). Remedies: `docker run --init` (injects *tini*), a real lightweight init, and the **exec form** (`CMD ["nginx", "-g", "daemon off;"]`) so the binary **is** PID 1 and properly receives SIGTERM.
</Note>

**Writing logs to files.** A container should log to **stdout/stderr**: that is what `docker logs` collects and what aggregators expect. Writing to `/var/log/тАж` inside the container makes the logs invisible and **inflates the writable layer**. Conversely, the default `json-file` driver is **unbounded** on the host: remember **rotation** (`max-size`, `max-file`).

**Not limiting resources.** Without `--memory` or `--cpus`, a leaking container can **take down the whole host** (OOM, saturated CPU). In production, set limits тАФ and sensible restart policies, not a blind `restart: always`.

**No health probe.** Without a `HEALTHCHECK`, the orchestrator thinks a container that has crashed internally is "healthy". And a `depends_on` **does not wait for the service to be ready**, only for it to have started: pair it with a `condition: service_healthy`.

<Note type="warning" title="Containers: cattle, not pets">
  SSHing into a container to "fix" it by hand, applying one-off patches, keeping it "because it is nicely configured": that is treating it like a VM. A container is **disposable** тАФ you fix the **image** (or the Compose file), then **recreate**. That is the whole point of reproducibility.
</Note>

## Data: lose nothing

**Putting durable data in the writable layer.** A database with no **volume**, and everything vanishes when the container is removed (seen at [step 4 of the lab](../tp-docker-wordpress/)). Persistent data тЗТ a **named volume** (or a controlled *bind mount*).

**Believing a named volume is safe.** A volume that is **named but unused** can fall to a `docker volume prune --all`; naming it does not excuse you from **backing it up** (details at [step 4 of the lab](../tp-docker-wordpress/)).

## Networking: don't expose yourself

<Note type="danger" title="Docker bypasses your firewall">
  Docker writes its own iptables/nftables rules **directly**. The counter-intuitive consequence: `-p 0.0.0.0:5432:5432` **actually opens the port to the outside**, even if UFW is supposed to block it тАФ hence thousands of databases exposed by accident. Remedies: publish on the **loopback** (`-p 127.0.0.1:5432:5432`) when external access is not wanted, keep the firewall consistent (e.g. *ufw-docker*), and publish only what must be.
</Note>

**Publishing useless ports.** An internal service (a database, a cache) need not be reachable from the host: leave it on the **service network**, without `-p` (our lab does **not** publish MySQL's port, in fact). Every published port is an open door.

## Recap

<Note type="success" title="The pre-production check-list">
  - No `docker.sock` mounted, no `--privileged`: isolation intact.
  - Non-privileged `USER`; secrets **at runtime**, never in the image.
  - **Official** images, **pinned** (digest), **scanned**.
  - `update && install && clean` in a **single** `RUN`; `.dockerignore`; `COPY` rather than `ADD`.
  - A **PID 1** that handles signals (`--init` / exec form); logs to **stdout**; resource **limits**; `HEALTHCHECK`.
  - Data in **volumes** (and **backed up**).
  - Publish (`-p`) **only** what is needed, and **not** on `0.0.0.0` by default.
</Note>
